How this calculator works

Compound interest adds earned interest back into the balance, allowing future interest to grow from both the original principal and accumulated earnings. Regular contributions can increase the final balance substantially over longer periods.

Use the result as a planning estimate. Real outcomes can vary because of fees, compounding rules, tax treatment, inflation and investment performance.

When this is useful

Use this calculator to compare savings goals, investment timelines, regular contribution amounts and different return assumptions. Try changing one input at a time to see which factor has the biggest effect.

Formula and interpretation

The core compound interest formula is final balance = principal × (1 + rate)time. When regular contributions are included, each contribution compounds from the time it is added.

The most important inputs are the starting amount, contribution amount, annual return, compounding frequency and years invested.

Worked example

Example: if you start with $10,000 and test a longer timeline, the later years often add more growth than the early years because previous interest can earn more interest.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Using rounded or guessed inputs when exact values are available.
  • Forgetting that fees, taxes, timing, and local rules can change real-world outcomes.
  • Treating the result as a final quote instead of a general estimate.
