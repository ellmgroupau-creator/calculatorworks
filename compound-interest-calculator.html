rate = annual rate ÷ 100
interest earned = final balance - principal

How to use compound interest results

Try changing the contribution amount, interest rate, and time period separately so you can see which assumption has the biggest effect on the final balance. Small changes to rate or time can create large differences because the interest earns interest over future periods.

Before relying on the result: Double-check whether your real account compounds daily, monthly, quarterly, or annually, and remember that fees, tax, inflation, and market volatility can reduce the actual value of future returns.

Why compound interest matters

Compound interest means interest is added to the balance and can then earn interest itself. Over short periods the difference may look small, but over years or decades compounding can become one of the biggest drivers of savings and investment growth.

The most important inputs are the starting amount, contribution amount, rate of return, compounding frequency, and time. The longer the time period, the more powerful compounding becomes. This is why starting earlier can sometimes matter more than starting with a larger initial balance.
